AI-visibility teardown · 2026-07-15

When the AI picks how you take payments, does it name your tool?

I asked Gemini and Perplexity how an indie SaaS founder should collect subscription payments — 24 times, four different ways — and counted who actually got named. Two incumbents ran the table. But the merchant-of-record tools indie hackers can't stop recommending to each other barely showed up, and that gap is the whole story.

What this is

Fewer founders open ten tabs to pick a payments stack now. A lot of them just ask an AI — "what should I use to take subscription payments for my SaaS?" — read the two or three names it hands back, and shortlist from those. So the real question for anyone selling billing or payments isn't "where do I rank on Google." It's when the engine answers, does it ever say my name?

The answers wobble run to run, so one screenshot proves nothing. I asked the same buyer questions 24 times across two engines (Gemini and Perplexity, 2026-07-02) and measured how often each product got named. That frequency is a number almost no founder in this space has ever seen about themselves.

The headline

Paddle was named in every single answer (100%), with Stripe right behind at 96%. The two incumbents own the AI's mouth here. This is the flip side of most categories I tear down — feature flags, backends, headless CMS — where the open-source or indie option out-names the leader. In payments, the establishment wins clean.

Then a steep cliff. Lemon Squeezy — the merchant-of-record darling of indie Twitter — lands at 62%. PayPal and FastSpring tie at 58%. And Polar, the newer open-source MoR that build-in-public founders keep evangelizing, sits at 8%: named in 2 of 24 answers. Nearly invisible.

"Visible in AI search" is not one number. It's one number per engine, and the engines disagree.

The part founders miss — the AI ignores the thing your peers keep telling you to use

If you've spent any time in build-in-public circles, you know the script: someone hits a Stripe tax headache, and ten people reply "switch to a merchant of record — Lemon Squeezy, Paddle, Polar, they handle the VAT for you." It's near-gospel. So you'd expect an AI trained on that open web to hand those names right back.

It mostly doesn't. Of the merchant-of-record tools founders evangelize, only Paddle actually converts that into AI mindshare (100%). Lemon Squeezy — arguably the loudest name in the indie MoR conversation — drops to 62%, behind plain Stripe. And Polar, the open-source challenger that gets breathless threads every few weeks, is a rounding error at 8%. The engine reaches for the biggest, oldest, most-written-about names — Paddle and Stripe — not the community favourite of the month.

The sources back this up. The single most-cited page across all these answers was paddle.com (25 times), then reddit.com (20) and youtube.com (15). Lemon Squeezy's own site was cited 10 times, Stripe's 9 — plenty of reads, not enough naming. If you're selling a payments tool, the lesson is blunt: being the answer indie Twitter gives is not the same as being the answer the AI gives. One is won in replies; the other is won in volume of durable, third-party writing across the whole web — and right now the incumbents own that.
