AI-visibility teardown · 2026-07-18

When the AI picks your auth provider, does it name the one you'd pick?

I asked Gemini and Perplexity which auth provider an indie SaaS founder should use — 24 times, four different ways — and counted who got named. The answer is a straight contradiction of what indie Twitter has been telling you for two years, and it's worth sitting with.

What this is

Auth is the loudest "just don't use the expensive one" argument in build-in-public. Every week someone posts the Auth0 bill, someone else replies "roll your own with Better Auth," and a third person swears by Clerk or Supabase. But fewer founders settle that argument by reading ten blog posts now — a lot of them just ask an AI, "which auth should I use for my SaaS?", read the two or three names it hands back, and shortlist from those.

So the real question for anyone selling authentication isn't "where do I rank on Google." It's when the engine answers, does it ever say my name? Answers wobble run to run, so one screenshot proves nothing. I asked the same buyer questions 24 times across two engines (Gemini and Perplexity, 2026-07-18) and measured how often each provider got named.

The headline

Auth0 was named in every single answer — 100%. Okta right behind at 96%, Clerk tied with it at 96%, then Supabase at 92%. The expensive incumbent that indie Twitter loves to dunk on is the one name the AI never leaves out.

Then the twist. Better Auth — the open-source library that's been the darling of the "stop paying for auth" crowd all year — lands at 17%. NextAuth / Auth.js, the default in half the Next.js tutorials ever written, comes in at 4%: named in 1 of 24 answers. The tools your feed evangelizes as the Auth0 escape hatch are nearly invisible to the engine a founder actually asks.

The part that should stop you — the more you publish, the less it seems to matter

Here's the finding I keep re-checking because it's so backwards. I counted which source domains the engines actually cited while writing these answers. The most-cited domain of the entire run was workos.com — 31 times, ahead of Reddit (22). Then a wall of challenger content sites: ssojet.com (21), descope.com (21), propelauth.com (20), supertokens.com (20). These companies are out-publishing everyone — running comparison posts, "best auth provider" roundups, alternatives pages, the whole GEO playbook.

And it barely moves their name. WorkOS, the single most-cited domain, got the product named 50% of the time. Descope: cited 21 times, named 38%. PropelAuth: cited 20 times, named 33%. SuperTokens: cited 20 times, named 17%. Meanwhile auth0.com was cited exactly once in the whole run, and okta.com three times — and those two were named 100% and 96%. The brand that publishes almost nothing about itself wins; the brands that publish the most sit mid-pack.

The lesson isn't "don't publish." It's that comparison content buys you citations, not recommendations. The engine reaches for the name with the most durable third-party mindshare — years of Reddit threads, tutorials, and Stack Overflow answers written by other people — and for auth that name is still Auth0. Your own alternatives page gets you cited as a footnote in an answer that recommends the incumbent. (I've watched this exact gap in six other categories now — being read is not being recommended.)

And the self-host tax shows up again

One more thread, because it keeps repeating. The open-source auth you can run yourself — Keycloak (46%), Ory (42%), SuperTokens (17%), Better Auth (17%) — all trail the managed options badly. But open source that comes managed, with a signup and a company behind it, keeps pace fine: Supabase Auth sits at 88%, right up with the leaders. That's the same split I found in hosting, where self-hosted Coolify came dead last. Open source doesn't lose to the AI. Self-host does — because self-host has no signup funnel and far less durable third-party writing for the engine to lean on. If your product is "run it yourself," visibility is a harder, separate fight.
