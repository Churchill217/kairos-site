AI-visibility teardown · 2026-07-07

"Just use Firebase" is what people say. It's not what the AI says.

I asked Gemini and Perplexity what backend an indie founder should build a SaaS on — 24 times, four different ways — and counted who actually got named. Firebase, the default everyone reaches for on reflex, came second. The open-source alternative, Supabase, was named in every single answer.

What this is

Ask a room of developers what backend to start a SaaS on and a chunk of them will say "Firebase" without thinking — it's the incumbent, the safe default, the one Google runs. But fewer builders open ten tabs to compare backends now. A lot of them just ask an AI — "what's the best backend-as-a-service for my startup?" — read the two or three names it hands back, and shortlist from those. So the real question for anyone selling in this space isn't "where do I rank on Google." It's when the engine answers, does it ever say my name?

The answers wobble run to run, so one screenshot proves nothing. I asked the same buyer questions 24 times across two engines (Gemini and Perplexity, 2026-07-07) and measured how often each product got named. That frequency is a number almost no founder in this space has ever seen about themselves.

The headline

Supabase — open source — was named in every single answer (100%), just ahead of Firebase (96%), the incumbent everyone assumes is the default. A second open-source option, Appwrite, came in at 83%. So the two names the AI reaches for most in this entire category are both self-hostable, and the heavyweight with Google behind it sits second.

This is the same pattern I keep hitting: when a developer asks an AI what to build on, the open-source name keeps coming out on or above the top. Backend is the clearest case of four verticals where that happened (also feature flags, vector databases, headless CMS). It's not an accident — it's what the open web has been written about. Supabase spent years being "the open-source Firebase alternative," and the engines learned exactly that phrasing.

Neon, Convex and Nhost each swing 25 points or more the same way. "Visible in AI search" is not one number. It's one number per engine, and the engines disagree hard.

The part founders miss — you're being recommended on turf you don't own

I also logged which web pages the engines actually opened to write these answers — 296 citations across 110 different domains. Here's the twist. Supabase got named in 100% of answers, but its own site, supabase.com, was cited just 3 times. Firebase got named in 96% of answers, and its own docs were cited zero times. The two most-recommended backends in the category are winning almost entirely on pages they don't control.

So where did the recommendation actually come from? The most-cited sources were a Reddit thread (19 times), a competitor's comparison blog on encore.dev (17), the makerkit.dev tutorial site (12), and Appwrite's own marketing site (12) — each cited more than either Supabase's or Firebase's homepage. Add YouTube (11), Medium (10) and dev.to (9) and the shape is clear: the engine builds its answer from Reddit, tutorials, videos and your rivals' "X vs Y" posts. You can write the best landing page in the world; the engine is quoting the internet's opinion of you, not your marketing copy. If you want to be named, you have to be in those conversations — not just have a great site.
