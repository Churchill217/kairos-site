When the AI picks where you deploy, does it name your platform?

I asked Gemini and Perplexity where an indie SaaS founder should host and deploy their app — 24 times, four different ways — and counted who got named. Two incumbents ran the table. But the self-hosted platform that build-in-public Twitter can't stop recommending came dead last, and that gap is the whole story.

What this is

Fewer founders read ten "Vercel vs Netlify vs Railway" blog posts now. A lot of them just ask an AI — "where should I deploy my SaaS?" — read the two or three names it hands back, and shortlist from those. So the real question for anyone selling hosting or deployment isn't "where do I rank on Google." It's when the engine answers, does it ever say my name?

The answers wobble run to run, so one screenshot proves nothing. I asked the same buyer questions 24 times across two engines (Gemini and Perplexity, 2026-07-16) and measured how often each platform got named. That frequency is a number almost no founder in this space has ever seen about themselves.

The headline

Vercel was named in nearly every answer (96%), with Netlify right behind at 92%. The two frontend-hosting incumbents own the AI's mouth here — and the challengers that keep up are the managed ones: Railway and Render both at 88%. This is a category where the establishment wins, like payments and search were.

Then the twist. Coolify — the self-hosted, open-source PaaS that indie founders evangelize as the way to escape Vercel's bill — lands at 12%: named in 3 of 24 answers, dead last of every tool on the board, behind even Koyeb. The loudest name in the "just self-host it" conversation is nearly invisible to the engine.

The part founders miss — the AI names the managed platform, not the one you self-host

There's a pattern across everything I've torn down: in developer infrastructure, the open-source option usually out-names the paid incumbent — Supabase over Firebase, Strapi on top of headless CMS, two open-source flag tools ahead of LaunchDarkly. So you'd expect hosting to follow suit, with the open-source self-host crowd riding high.

It doesn't — and the reason is sharp. The open-source winners in those other categories were all managed services: you sign up, they run it, there's a company and a pricing page and a pile of "how I deployed on X" writeups. Coolify is open source too, but you host it yourself — and self-host has no signup funnel, no managed-vs-managed comparison content, far less durable third-party writing. So the engine, reaching for the most-written-about names, reaches for Vercel, Netlify, Railway, Render — every one a managed platform. Open source doesn't lose here. Self-host does.

The sources make it concrete. The most-cited page across these answers was dev.to (30 times), then northflank.com (19) and medium.com (18) — third-party tutorials and one vendor's own comparison posts. Here's the kicker: Northflank writes so much comparison content that its domain was the second-most-cited source in the whole run — yet Northflank the product only got named 38% of the time, mid-pack. Being read is not being recommended. If you sell hosting, the lesson is blunt: the engine names the platform with the most durable third-party writing about it — not the one your peers are evangelizing this month, and not the one that writes the most about itself.
