AI-visibility teardown · the synthesis · 2026-07-17

Seven categories in, the AI's real bias isn't open source vs paid. It's managed vs invisible.

I've now asked Gemini and Perplexity who to buy in seven developer categories, hundreds of answers each, and counted who actually gets named. The clean "open source always wins" story is wrong. The real pattern is sharper, and it decides whether the engine ever says your name.

What this is

Each of the seven teardowns below is the same experiment run on a different category: take the questions a founder would actually ask an AI ("best headless CMS for a small SaaS?", "where should I deploy my app?"), ask them 24 times across two engines, and count how often each product gets named. The answers wobble run to run, so I never trust one screenshot — I measure a frequency. Every number on this page is pulled straight from a published teardown you can open and check.

Seven categories is enough to stop looking at them one at a time and ask the better question: across all of them, what actually decides who the AI names?

The headline

In four of seven categories the open-source option out-named the paid incumbent. In three the establishment won clean. So "open source always beats the incumbent" was never a law. Look closer at the four wins and the three losses together, though, and one line separates them: the AI names the product that is both easy to sign up for and heavily written about by other people. Managed and well-documented gets named. Self-hosted, niche, or brand-new goes invisible — no matter how much your peers love it.

Three laws that held across all seven

1. Managed open source out-names the paid incumbent — but self-host loses.

The four open-source winners — Supabase, Strapi, Flagsmith, Qdrant — have one thing in common that's easy to miss: every one is a managed service. You can self-host them, but you can also click "sign up" and let them run it, which means each has a pricing page, an onboarding funnel, and a mountain of "how I built on X" writeups feeding the engine. The category where open source lost the loudest was hosting: Coolify, the self-hosted PaaS that build-in-public Twitter can't stop recommending, landed dead last at 12%. Same license, opposite result. Open source doesn't win because it's open — it wins when it's managed and written-about. Self-host has no funnel and thin third-party coverage, so the engine skips it.

2. Being cited is not being named.

The engines footnote sources. Founders assume that if their domain is in the citations, they're winning. They're not the same thing. In hosting, Northflank's domain was the second-most-cited source in the entire run — its own comparison content — yet the Northflank product only got named 38% of the time, mid-pack. In backend, Firebase's own docs were cited zero times, and the answer was assembled from Reddit threads and third-party blogs the vendors don't own. The engine reads your competitor's tutorial and recommends whoever that tutorial talks about most. Writing a lot about yourself doesn't move the needle; being the subject of what other people write does.

3. The answer your peers give is not the answer the AI gives.

Every category had an indie-Twitter darling that the engine underweighted or ignored. Coolify (12%) in hosting. Polar (8%) and Lemon Squeezy (62%) in payments — the merchant-of-record tools indie founders evangelize monthly. The newest, loudest name in the group chat is usually the one the AI hasn't caught up to, because durable third-party writing takes months to accumulate. If your growth plan is "get talked about on X," know that the engine is reading a slower, older, more boring corpus — and that's the one deciding whether a buyer ever hears your name.

And one caveat that runs under all three: "visible in AI" is not one number. The engines disagree hard. Google Cloud Run was named 42% of the time by Gemini and 0% by Perplexity in the hosting run; Cloudflare flipped the other way. It's one score per engine, and you have to measure each.

So what do you do with this?

If you sell into one of these categories, the playbook falls out of the pattern: make yourself easy to try (a real managed on-ramp, not just a repo), get other people writing comparisons that include you (the citations that matter are never your own), and measure your named-rate per engine instead of guessing from one screenshot. Most owners have never seen that number for their own product. A lot of them are invisible in there and have no idea yet.
