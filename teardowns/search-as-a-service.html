When the AI picks your search API, does it name you?

I asked Gemini and Perplexity which hosted search API an indie founder should add to their app — 24 times, four different ways — and counted who actually got named. This time the paid leader held first place. But the story underneath the table is the interesting part.

What this is

Fewer buyers open ten browser tabs to compare tools now. A lot of them just ask an AI — "what's the best search API to add to my SaaS?" — read the two or three names it hands back, and shortlist from those. So the real question for anyone selling in this category isn't "where do I rank on Google." It's when the engine answers, does it ever say my name?

The answers wobble run to run, so one screenshot proves nothing. I asked the same buyer questions 24 times across two engines (Gemini and Perplexity, 2026-07-09) and measured how often each product got named. That frequency is the number almost no founder in this space has ever seen about themselves.

The headline

Algolia — the category's best-known paid incumbent — actually held first place, named in every single answer. In my last few teardowns the loud paid leader kept losing to open source. Here it didn't. So "open source always out-names the incumbent" was never a law — it's category by category, and this is the honest counter-case.

Meilisearch (open source) came a close second at 96%, Elasticsearch third at 92%, and Typesense (open source) fourth at 79%. A tight, credible top four — with the paid option still on top.

The part founders miss — cited is not the same as recommended

I also logged which web pages the engines actually read to write these answers. Here's the twist. The most-cited source of all was meilisearch.com (31 times), then typesense.org (20) — the two open-source docs sites. Algolia's own site (algolia.com) was cited only 12 times. The open-source projects got quoted roughly three times as often.

And yet Algolia was still named first in every answer. That's the lesson worth sitting with: being cited — having your docs quoted as a source — is not the same as being recommended. Brand prominence carried Algolia to the top of the list even though the engines were reading the competitors' pages to get there. Getting the AI to read you and getting it to name you are two different fights, and you can win one while losing the other.
