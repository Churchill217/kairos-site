The experiment I'm running now

Businesses spent twenty years fighting for the top of Google. But more and more, the search ends somewhere else: a person asks ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity "who's the best ___ near me," reads the few names it hands back, and never scrolls a results page at all. Which raises a question almost nobody is measuring: when the AI answers, does it ever say your name?

That's the test. I built a tool that asks the engines, over and over, what they recommend in a category, and counts how often each business actually gets named. Not one lucky screenshot — the answers wobble run to run, so a single grab proves nothing. A frequency, measured across many asks and several engines. Most owners have never seen this number. A lot of them are invisible in there and have no idea yet.

New — the synthesis: Seven teardowns in, here's the actual pattern in who the AI names. Open source out-named the paid incumbent in four of seven developer categories, but the real rule is sharper: the engine names what's managed and heavily written-about, and ignores what you self-host — no matter how much your peers love it. Three laws of AI visibility, with every number.

Latest single teardown: Does the AI name your auth provider? — Auth0, the incumbent indie Twitter loves to dunk on, was named in 100% of answers (Okta and Clerk right behind at 96%). Better Auth, the current "stop paying for auth" darling, landed at 17%; NextAuth at 4%. And the challengers who publish the most comparison content (WorkOS cited 31×) still got named half as often as a brand cited once. Comparison content buys citations, not recommendations.

The full library: hosting (Vercel and Netlify owned it; self-hosted Coolify came dead last), payments (Paddle and Stripe owned it; indie-favourite Lemon Squeezy and Polar fell behind), backend-as-a-service (open-source Supabase out-named Firebase), feature flags (two open-source tools beat the paid heavyweight, which came fourth), headless CMS (Strapi topped it — but read is not named), search API (paid Algolia held #1, yet OSS docs cited 3x more), vector databases (the paid leader came third). Eight categories in, the pattern splits clean: in developer infrastructure the open-source name usually out-names the paid incumbent — but where the incumbents are entrenched (payments, hosting, auth) or the challenger is self-hosted, the establishment owns the answer.
